Improving Kitchen Prep Time (KPT) Prediction through Signal Decomposition and Operational Signal Analysis
Problem Understanding
Kitchen Prep Time (KPT) is a key driver of ETA accuracy and rider assignment efficiency. Inaccurate estimation leads to early rider arrival, increased wait time, rider idle cost, fluctuating ETAs, and poor customer experience.
In real-world systems, KPT is often derived from merchant-marked Food Order Ready (FOR) signals. However, these signals can be noisy due to operational behavior and hidden kitchen load.
Using a simulated order-level delivery dataset, we aimed to decompose total delivery duration into operational components and isolate the kitchen preparation signal for deeper analysis.
Rather than modifying the prediction model directly, this solution focuses on improving upstream signal clarity.

Methodology: Signal Decomposition Approach
Since explicit Food Ready timestamps were not available in the dataset, KPT was estimated by decomposing total delivery duration into:
Total Delivery Duration = Estimated Travel Time + Estimated Kitchen Prep Time
Travel time was approximated using:
Delivery distance


Traffic level


Estimated_KPT = Delivery_Duration_Minutes – Estimated_Travel_Time
This allowed isolation of the preparation component for operational analysis.

Signal Reliability & Operational Insights
1. Rush Detection (Kitchen Load Estimation)
Using hour-of-day aggregation, we identified time windows where average Estimated_KPT increased significantly.
This indicates:
 • Kitchen congestion
 • Order backlog pressure
 • Potential underestimation of prep time during peak hours
A Rush Index can be derived from hourly deviation patterns to dynamically adjust KPT during high-load periods.

2. Merchant-Level Variability (Reliability Proxy)
For each restaurant:
 • Average Estimated_KPT
 • Standard deviation of Estimated_KPT
High variance suggests:
 • Inconsistent preparation patterns
 • Potential signal instability
 • Operational inefficiency
This can act as a Merchant Reliability Score proxy, where lower variance indicates more stable prep behavior.

3. Traffic vs Prep Signal Separation
By comparing:
 • Average Travel Time by traffic level
 • Average Estimated_KPT by traffic level
We observed that traffic primarily impacts travel component, not prep component.
This validates the decomposition approach and helps prevent mixing travel noise with kitchen signal.

Scalable Implementation Concept
In production systems:
Signal Layer:
 • Merchant-marked readiness timestamps
 • Rider arrival timestamps
 • Historical delay residuals
Reliability Layer:
 • Merchant stability scoring
 • Rush multiplier adjustment
Enriched Signal:
 • Cleaned KPT fed into existing prediction model
This preserves the current modeling architecture while improving input fidelity.

Expected Impact
• Reduced rider wait time
 • Improved ETA accuracy (P50 / P90)
 • Lower idle rider cost
 • Better alignment of dispatch timing
Even small improvements in prep-time accuracy can significantly improve operational efficiency at scale.

Conclusion
This solution demonstrates how decomposing delivery duration into travel and preparation components enables signal-level improvement without changing the core model.
By identifying rush patterns and merchant variability, we improve upstream signal reliability — leading to better KPT estimation, improved rider assignment, and enhanced ETA stability.
